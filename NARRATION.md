# Narration Script — Ankylosaurus Website Presentation

Purpose: A concise, clear spoken narration to present your Ankylosaurus website (GEOL201L). Use this as your script and as speaker notes while recording the narrated website.

Total length target: 6–8 minutes (recommended for classroom judging). Keep each slide 45–70 seconds.

---

## Opening (0:00–0:30)
- Hello — "Hi, I’m Joshua Lopez. This is my GEOL201L project: The Ankylosaurus, presented from a historical geology perspective."
- One-line objective: "I will show when and where Ankylosaurus lived, what its anatomy tells us about how it survived, how fossils inform our knowledge, and how it went extinct at the end of the Cretaceous."
- Visual cue: Home hero image on screen. Speak clearly, smile, and pause for 1–2 seconds after the objective.

---

## Slide: Time Period (0:30–1:20)
- Script: "Ankylosaurus lived about 68 to 66 million years ago during the very end of the Cretaceous Period. It lived on the western landmass of North America, near river floodplains preserved today as the Hell Creek and Lance Formations. The world then was warmer, with higher sea levels and different plant communities."
- Cite on-screen: "Source: Encyclopaedia Britannica" (point to the Time Period sidebar reference).
- Visual cue: map / Time Period figure. Speak at a measured pace; emphasize the dates and formations.

---

## Slide: Characteristics (1:20–2:10)
- Script: "Ankylosaurus was built for defense: rows of bony armor embedded in the skin, extra-thick rings protecting the neck, and a massive tail club. It was slow but extremely well protected — the tail could be used actively as a weapon."
- Call out a measurement: "About 6–8 meters long, 4–8 tons, tail club ~30–45 kg." Keep numbers brief and simple.
- Cite on-screen: "Sources: Dinopedia (image), Wikipedia (skull/fossil notes)."
- Visual cue: show size comparison then skull fossil — speak as the images appear. Avoid jargon; if you use a term like "osteoderm," explain it quickly: "osteoderms — bony plates in the skin." 

---

## Slide: Discovery (2:10–3:00)
- Script: "We know Ankylosaurus from only a few incomplete fossils. The first specimen was found in 1906 by Barnum Brown; more fragments came later. Because specimens are rare, each fossil helps scientists piece together the animal’s anatomy and life."
- Cite on-screen: "Sources: National Geographic Kids; historical references (Brown, 1908) noted in Sources page."
- Visual cue: timeline box. Keep sentences short and concrete.

---

## Slide: Evolution (3:00–3:50)
- Script: "Ankylosaurs evolved over roughly 100 million years from relatives with lighter armor. Over time some developed tail clubs that turned defense from passive to active. Ankylosaurus shows the peak of that trend: thick skull, integrated armor, and a big club."
- Cite on-screen: "Sources: DinosaurPictures.org; Dinopedia; Britannica for evolutionary context."
- Visual cue: evolutionary timeline graphic.

---

## Slide: Extinction (3:50–4:40)
- Script: "All non-avian dinosaurs, including Ankylosaurus, went extinct 66 million years ago. The primary cause is a large asteroid impact at Chicxulub marked worldwide by an iridium layer, shocked minerals, and glass spherules. The immediate loss of sunlight and collapse of plant life removed food sources for large herbivores."
- Cite on-screen: "Sources: Britannica; National summaries on the Sources page."
- Visual cue: K-Pg explanation box and Earth-impact diagram.

---

## Slide: Sources & Image Credits (4:40–5:10)
- Script: "All factual claims and images are cited on the Sources page. Key references include Encyclopaedia Britannica, Wikipedia, Dinopedia, National Geographic Kids, DinosaurPictures.org, and Mini Museum materials. Please see the Sources page for full APA citations."
- Visual cue: open Sources page and point to the image credits table.

---

## Closing / Conclusions (5:10–5:40)
- Script: "In summary: Ankylosaurus was a heavily armored, slow-moving plant-eater that relied on armor and a tail club for defense, lived in the last two million years of the Cretaceous in western North America, and went extinct with the end-Cretaceous collapse. Thank you — I’m happy to answer questions."
- Visual cue: final slide; show your name and course information.

---

## Delivery & Rubric Alignment — quick tips
- Delivery: Speak clearly, project your voice, and pace yourself so visuals change as you reach key sentences. Aim for calm, even pacing with natural pauses.
- Word choice / grammar: Use short sentences. Avoid excessive technical terms; when necessary, define them quickly in one clause.
- Legibility & simplicity: Keep the browser zoom at a level where text and images are readable from a distance. When recording, use full-screen mode and pause on each slide for at least 10–12 seconds.
- Slides / visuals count: Keep to 6–8 main screens (Home, Time Period, Characteristics, Discovery, Evolution, Extinction, Sources, Closing).
- Substantiation: When you state a fact (dates, measurements), show the Sources page or the sidebar citation briefly so judges can verify.
